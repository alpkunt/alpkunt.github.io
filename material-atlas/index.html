How to read this. Each material carries twelve measured properties — density, Young's modulus, tensile strength, hardness, thermal and electrical conductivity, melting point, thermal expansion, specific heat, fracture toughness, ductility and rough cost. Properties that span many orders of magnitude are log‑transformed, then every axis is standardized so none dominates. Affinity between two materials is a Gaussian function of their distance across all axes (100 = identical property profile). Families come from k‑means; the flattened map from multidimensional scaling.

A caveat worth measuring. These are representative handbook values, not a single certified specimen. Real materials vary with alloy, temper, grade and processing — "steel" or "aluminium" spans a whole family of its own. Read the numbers as typical and comparative rather than exact. Method & Data

The maths behind the map.

Real, tabulated engineering properties — then some plain math on top. Here's exactly how the atlas is built, and the numbers it runs on.

How it was built
  1. Measure — each material carries 12 real properties (the table below).
  2. Log & standardize — log wide-ranging axes, then z‑score every axis.
  3. Distance — Euclidean distance across all 780 material pairs.
  4. Affinity — distance mapped to 0–100 by a Gaussian falloff.
  5. Cluster — k‑means (k = 6) groups the similar materials.
  6. Flatten — classical MDS drops 12‑D down to the 2‑D property map.
Sources & honesty

Unlike a taste chart, these numbers are real measured quantities — representative handbook values for density, stiffness, strength, conductivity and the rest. They still carry uncertainty: a property like strength or cost swings widely with grade, temper and market.

A single label also flattens a whole sub-family — “steel” alone spans mild to tool grades. So read every value as typical and comparative, not a certified spec. Everything downstream is plain, reproducible math on exactly the table below: change a number and the whole atlas re‑draws.
